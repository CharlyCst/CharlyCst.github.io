Research Statement

Software is going through a trust crisis. Our society critically relies on software, from the apps on our smartphones to the systems that power our businesses and governments, but software itself is getting out of control. Nowadays, the simplest applications embed hundreds of dependencies written by thousands of independent developers, while our operating systems, kernels, and hypervisors are exposing an ever-growing attack surface that results in a never-ending flow of critical vulnerabilities.

Our systems, whose fundamental design originates from the ’70s, are unable to face the complexity of the modern world: trust in the all-mighty privileged code is eroded, compute is moving to the public cloud, and our applications are exponentially growing in size. The abstractions baked in our systems, processes and virtual machines, can no longer keep up with the increasingly complex trust relationships between software components.

The way we build and compose software is in desperate need for an update. My research focus on developing new isolation abstractions and mechanisms to power our systems for the next 50 years. I rethink how isolation should work, from programming languages and compilers all the way down to operating systems and hypervisors.
